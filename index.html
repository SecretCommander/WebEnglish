What are Singular Nouns?

Singular nouns refer to one person, place, thing, or idea. They are used when talking about a single item or entity, usually started with a/an.

LEARN MORE →

What are Plural Nouns?

Plural nouns refer to more than one person, place, thing, or idea. They're typically formed by adding -s or -es to the singular form.

Singular Plural Noun

Understanding the difference is crucial for proper grammar and communication

There are several general rules for changing singular nouns into their plural form

Adding '-s' (The Most Common Rule)

Most nouns form their plural by simply adding the suffix -s.

Examples:

  • cat → cats
  • book → books
  • table → tables

Adding '-es'

This rule applies to nouns ending in -s, -ss, -sh, -ch, -x, or -z.

Examples:

  • bus → buses
  • brush → brushes
  • watch → watches
  • box → boxes
  • quiz → quizzes

Nouns Ending in '-y'

If the letter before '-y' is a consonant, change the -y to -i and then add -es.

Examples:

  • baby → babies
  • city → cities
  • story → stories

If the letter before '-y' is a vowel (a, e, i, o, u), simply add -s.

Examples:

  • boy → boys
  • day → days
  • key → keys

Nouns Ending in '-f' or '-fe'

For some nouns ending in -f or -fe, change the -f or -fe to -v and then add -es.

Examples:

  • leaf → leaves
  • knife → knives
  • wife → wives

Some words just add -s.

Examples:

  • roof → roofs
  • chief → chiefs
  • cliff → cliffs

Nouns Ending in '-o'

Some nouns ending in -o form their plural by adding -es.

Examples:

  • potato → potatoes
  • tomato → tomatoes
  • hero → heroes

Others only add -s, especially for loanwords or abbreviations.

Examples:

  • photo → photos
  • piano → pianos
  • radio → radios

Irregular Plural Noun

Some nouns have plural forms that don't follow the general rules above. These must be memorized.

Countable Nouns

Have both singular and plural forms. We can use numbers with them

Examples:

  • one apple
  • two peoples
  • Cats

Uncountable Nouns

Don't typically have a plural form (or their plural form is rarely used). They are always considered singular and cannot be counted with numbers.

Examples:

  • water
  • information
  • advice
  • furniture
  • money
  • happiness

To express quantity for uncountable nouns, we use quantifying phrases (e.g., a glass of water, a piece of information).

Vowel Changes

Examples:

  • man → men
  • woman → women
  • foot → feet
  • tooth → teeth
  • goose → geese
  • mouse → mice
  • louse → lice

Different/Special Endings

Examples:

  • child → children
  • ox → oxen

Same Singular and Plural Form

Examples:

  • sheep → sheep
  • fish → fish (though fishes can be used to refer to different species of fish)
  • deer → deer
  • series → series
  • species → species

Nouns of Foreign Origin (Latin/Greek)

Examples:

  • cactus → cacti (or cactuses)
  • nucleus → nuclei
  • fungus → fungi (or funguses)
  • syllabus → syllabi (or syllabuses)
  • criterion → criteria
  • phenomenon → phenomena
  • datum → data (though data is often treated as singular in everyday use)

Subject-Verb Agreement

In English, the subject and verb must agree in number. This means that a singular subject takes a singular verb, and a plural subject takes a plural verb.

1. Adding '-s' to Verbs in Present Tense

In Simple Present Tense, for third-person singular subjects (he, she, it, or singular nouns), the verb takes '-s' or '-es'. For plural subjects (and I, you, we, they), the base form is used.

Singular Subject + Singular Verb: - The boy runs quickly. (Not 'run') - She sings beautifully. (Not 'sing') - My mother cooks dinner every night. (Not 'cook') - This book contains interesting stories. (Not 'contain') Plural Subject + Plural Verb: - The boys run quickly. (Not 'runs') - They sing beautifully. (Not 'sings') - My parents cook dinner every night. (Not 'cooks') - These books contain interesting stories. (Not 'contains')

2. Using 'To Be' (Am, Is, Are, Was, Were)

The verb 'to be' changes depending on the subject and tense.

Present Tense: - I am a student. - He/She/It is happy. - The cat is sleeping. - My brother is tall. - We/You/They are friends. - The cats are sleeping. - My brothers are tall. Past Tense: - I/He/She/It was there. - The dog was barking. - That day was hot. - We/You/They were tired. - The dogs were barking. - Those days were hot.

3. Using 'To Have' (Has, Have)

The verb 'have' also changes depending on the subject in the present tense, but 'had' is used for all subjects in the past.

Present Tense: - I/You/We/They have a car. - The students have completed their assignments. - He/She/It has a car. - My sister has completed her assignment. Past Tense: - All the students had finished. - The teacher had explained everything.

4. Prepositional Phrases Between Subject and Verb

When a prepositional phrase comes between the subject and the verb, the verb must agree with the subject, not the noun in the phrase.

- The box of chocolates is on the table. (Not 'are') - The student with all the books is very smart. (Not 'are') - The flowers in the vase are beautiful. (Not 'is')

5. Nouns Joined by 'And'

When two or more subjects are joined by 'and', they usually take a plural verb.

- John and Mary are going to the party. - A pen and a pencil are needed for the test. - The dog and the cat sleep in the same bed.

6. Nouns Joined by 'Or' or 'Nor'

When subjects are joined by 'or' or 'nor', the verb agrees with the subject closest to it.

- Either John or Mary is responsible. - Neither the students nor the teacher is ready. - Neither the teacher nor the students are ready.

7. Indefinite Pronouns

Some are always singular, some always plural, and some depend on the context.

Always Singular: - Everyone is here. - Each of the students has a book. - Nobody knows the answer. Always Plural: - Both of them are coming. - Many believe in ghosts. Singular or Plural Based on Context: - All of the cake is eaten. - All of the cookies are eaten. - Some of the water is spilled. - Some of the students are absent.

8. Collective Nouns

Collective nouns can be singular or plural depending on whether the group acts as one or as individuals.

As a Single Unit (Singular): - The team is ready for the game. - My family is going on vacation. As Individuals (Plural - esp. in British English): - The team are arguing among themselves. - My family are discussing their plans.

9. Always Plural Nouns

Some nouns are always plural, even if they refer to one item with two parts or groups of people.

- The scissors are on the table. - My trousers are too long. - The police are investigating the case. - His data are accurate.

10. Singular-Looking but Plural Nouns

Some nouns end in '-s' but are treated as singular grammatically.

- What news is there? - Mathematics is my favorite subject. - Measles is a contagious disease. - The United States is a large country.
